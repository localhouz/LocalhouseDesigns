ERP Lite: designing an operational
decision surface.

A design and UX case study on turning ERP-heavy operations data into a readable front end for floor teams, planners, and leadership without rebuilding the system of record.

Written by Steven Rausch from Localhouse Designs in Broken Arrow, Oklahoma.

ERP Lite started from a simple observation: most ERP screens are good at transactions, but not especially good at decisions. They store the data, enforce the workflow, and keep the business moving. What they don't usually do is give a floor lead or planner a fast, readable answer to "what needs attention right now?"

That gap is where a lot of internal frustration lives. The data exists, but the interface still makes people interpret too much of it for themselves. ERP Lite is a study in what happens when the front end is allowed to be opinionated about attention.

The design problem

The design problem wasn't "how do we build another dashboard?" It was "how do we reduce interpretation time without pretending the ERP should be replaced?"

That changes the framing. Instead of starting from charts or reports, ERP Lite starts from operational questions:

  • What is running, idle, down, or in setup?
  • Which exceptions actually matter right now?
  • What view does a floor lead need that leadership doesn't?
  • How can the front end stay stable even if the ERP connector changes?

Once the problem is framed that way, the right interface starts to look less like a reporting screen and more like a decision surface.

What a decision surface means here

A decision surface isn't just a prettier dashboard. It's a front end that narrows the field of attention. Instead of making the user scan menus, tabs, and status codes, it shows the handful of states that move the day forward.

For ERP Lite, that meant designing around status, exception, and role:

  • Status tells the user what condition the operation is in now.
  • Exceptions tell the user what is off-track and needs action.
  • Role views tell the user what matters to them without drowning them in someone else's context.

Why the adapter-first model matters

The most important architectural choice wasn't visual. It was the adapter contract. The front end only consumes one normalized model: machine, line, status, shift, throughput, work order, and last heartbeat. That gives the UI a stable shape even when the upstream ERP changes.

That matters because real ERP environments are inconsistent. SAP, Epicor, and SyteLine all expose different nouns, different APIs, and different assumptions. If the UI is tightly coupled to each one, every connector becomes a redesign problem. ERP Lite avoids that by making connector variability an adapter concern, not a UI concern.

Why the first dataset was IoT, not ERP

We chose a public IoT manufacturing dataset for the first working demo because it behaves like a real floor: noisy, time-sensitive, and imperfect. That made it a better stress test for the interaction model than a clean, static spreadsheet would have been.

The goal was never to pretend IoT data is ERP data. The goal was to prove that the interface could survive real operational messiness while staying legible.

The UX layer: floor, planner, leadership

One of the core UX ideas in ERP Lite is that the same data shouldn't be rendered the same way for every role.

  • The floor view prioritizes status and machine-level state.
  • The planner view prioritizes exceptions, stale rows, and blocked work.
  • The leadership view compresses the system into uptime, bottlenecks, and risk summaries.

That's a small shift with a big effect. It makes the interface feel designed for decisions instead of designed for database completeness.

What this proves

ERP Lite proves that a vendor-agnostic operations front end is realistic if the design starts from readability and the architecture starts from normalization. The ERP remains the system of record. The interface becomes the system of interpretation.

That's the difference between software that stores the operation and software that helps people run it.
